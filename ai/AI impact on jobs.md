# What is the impact of AI to the human society? it will cause mass unemployment?

AI will definitely change human society a lot, but **“mass unemployment” is only one possible outcome**—and historically, technology usually causes **job transformation more than permanent job loss**. Let’s break it down clearly.

---

## 1. What AI is already doing to society

AI mainly automates **cognitive tasks** (thinking, analyzing, writing), similar to how machines previously automated **physical labor**.

Examples:

* Accounting automation
* Customer support chatbots
* Software code generation
* Medical image analysis
* Autonomous vehicles (eventually)

Unlike past automation, AI affects **both blue-collar and white-collar jobs**.

---

## 2. Jobs most at risk

AI replaces tasks that are:

**1️⃣ Repetitive**
**2️⃣ Rule-based**
**3️⃣ Digital**

Examples likely to shrink:

* Data entry
* Basic programming
* Customer support agents
* Paralegals doing document review
* Translation
* Simple design/content writing

Studies from organizations like McKinsey & Company and International Monetary Fund estimate **30–40% of tasks in many jobs could be automated**.

But note: **tasks**, not entire jobs.

---

## 3. Jobs that will grow

AI also creates demand for new roles.

Examples:

### AI-related jobs

* AI engineers
* Machine learning engineers
* AI safety researchers
* AI product managers

### Human-centric jobs

Jobs requiring empathy, leadership, or physical presence:

* Healthcare workers
* Therapists
* Teachers
* Skilled trades (electricians, mechanics)

### AI-augmented jobs

Many professionals will **use AI rather than be replaced by it**:

* Doctors using AI diagnosis
* Lawyers using AI research tools
* Software engineers using AI coding assistants

---

## 4. Historical pattern of technology

Past technological revolutions caused similar fears.

| Technology            | Initial Fear             | Long-term Result     |
| --------------------- | ------------------------ | -------------------- |
| Industrial Revolution | Machines replace workers | Huge economic growth |
| Computers             | Office jobs disappear    | New digital economy  |
| Internet              | Retail collapse          | E-commerce boom      |

Economists call this **creative destruction**, a concept from Joseph Schumpeter.

Old jobs disappear → new industries emerge.

---

## 5. The real short-term risk: job disruption

The biggest challenge is **speed**.

AI is developing **much faster** than past technologies.

Potential problems:

* Workers need retraining
* Some professions may shrink rapidly
* Income inequality could increase
* Transition period may create unemployment

This is why governments and organizations like World Economic Forum discuss:

* reskilling programs
* universal basic income (UBI)
* shorter work weeks

---

## 6. Possible long-term futures

Experts debate several scenarios.

### Scenario A — AI boosts productivity (optimistic)

Humans work **less but earn more**.

Example:

* 4-day work week
* AI assistants for every profession

---

### Scenario B — inequality increases

AI benefits mainly companies and capital owners.

Possible effects:

* fewer middle-class jobs
* wealth concentration

---

### Scenario C — post-work society

If AI eventually does most labor:

* humans focus on creativity
* science
* art
* exploration

This is sometimes called the **“abundance economy.”**

---

## 7. Important insight

AI will likely **change jobs more than eliminate them**.

For example:

**Software engineer (today)**

* write code

**Software engineer (future)**

* design systems
* review AI-generated code
* focus on architecture

The role evolves rather than disappears.

---

✅ **Key takeaway**

AI will likely cause:

* short-term disruption
* job transformation
* new industries
* higher productivity

But **mass permanent unemployment is unlikely** unless society fails to adapt.

# Explain more on universal basic income (UBI).

**Universal Basic Income (UBI)** is a policy where the government gives **every citizen a fixed amount of money regularly**, regardless of their job status, income, or wealth.

The idea has gained attention again because of automation and AI potentially replacing some jobs.

---

# 1. What Universal Basic Income means

UBI has three key characteristics:

### 1️⃣ Universal

Everyone receives it:

* rich or poor
* employed or unemployed
* no eligibility checks

### 2️⃣ Unconditional

You **do not need to work** or meet conditions.

### 3️⃣ Regular payments

Usually:

* monthly
* enough to cover **basic living costs**

Example hypothetical UBI:

| Country   | Monthly UBI |
| --------- | ----------- |
| US        | $1000       |
| Singapore | S$800       |
| EU        | €900        |

These numbers are just examples.

---

# 2. Why UBI is being discussed now

Automation and AI may reduce demand for some jobs.

Organizations like International Monetary Fund and World Economic Forum have studied how automation could affect employment.

UBI is proposed as a **safety net** if:

* robots do most labor
* productivity increases
* fewer workers are needed

The logic:

> If technology creates wealth, society should share it.

---

# 3. Example: how UBI would work

Imagine a country with:

* 10 million adults
* UBI = $1000/month

Annual cost:

```
10,000,000 × $1000 × 12
= $120 billion
```

Funding could come from:

* taxes
* wealth taxes
* AI/robot taxes
* replacing existing welfare programs

---

# 4. Real-world experiments

Several countries have tested UBI-like programs.

### 🇫🇮 Finland experiment (2017–2018)

Conducted by Social Insurance Institution of Finland.

* 2000 unemployed people
* €560/month
* unconditional payment

Results:

* slightly improved happiness
* reduced stress
* **no major drop in job seeking**

---

### 🇺🇸 Alaska Permanent Fund

Run by the Alaska Permanent Fund.

Citizens receive yearly payments from oil revenue.

Typical payment:

```
$1000–$2000 per year
```

It’s not full UBI, but it proves **resource dividends** can work.

---

### 🇨🇦 Canada pilot

Run in Ontario (2017).

Payments:

* about $17,000/year for individuals

Initial results suggested:

* improved mental health
* better employment stability

The program ended early for political reasons.

---

# 5. Arguments supporting UBI

### ✔️ Reduces poverty

People always have basic income.

### ✔️ Handles automation

Workers displaced by AI still have income.

### ✔️ Encourages entrepreneurship

People may start businesses without fear of starvation.

### ✔️ Less bureaucracy

Traditional welfare requires:

* eligibility checks
* paperwork
* administration

UBI is simpler.

---

# 6. Criticism of UBI

### ❌ Very expensive

For large countries, cost could be **trillions**.

Example estimate from Brookings Institution:

Full UBI in the US could cost **$3 trillion/year**.

---

### ❌ Possible reduced work incentive

Critics argue some people may choose not to work.

Supporters say most people still want:

* higher income
* purpose
* career growth

---

### ❌ Inflation risk

If everyone gets cash, prices might increase.

But economists debate how strong this effect would be.

---

# 7. Famous supporters of UBI

Many economists and technologists support exploring UBI.

Examples:

* Milton Friedman (negative income tax concept)
* Andrew Yang (US presidential candidate)
* Sam Altman (CEO of OpenAI)

They argue automation will create so much productivity that society should distribute some of it.

---

# 8. Alternatives to UBI

Governments may choose other solutions.

### Negative income tax

Proposed by Milton Friedman.

People earning below a threshold receive money from the government.

---

### Job guarantee programs

Government ensures everyone who wants work **has a job**.

---

### Reskilling programs

Workers retrain into new industries.

Example: AI, robotics, green energy.

---

# 9. Key debate about UBI

The fundamental question:

> If machines produce most wealth, who owns the income?

Two possibilities:

1️⃣ Only companies benefit
2️⃣ Society shares the gains

UBI is one proposed mechanism for sharing.

---

✅ **Important insight**

UBI is not just about unemployment.

It’s about how society distributes wealth in an **AI-driven economy**.
